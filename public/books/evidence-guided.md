# Evidence-Guided: Creating High Impact Products in the Age of Uncertainty

## Why I Read It

I picked this up after years of working with roadmaps that were really just stakeholder wish lists dressed up in a quarterly template. I knew the system was broken — but I didn't have a clean replacement. Itamar Gilad did.

## The Core Idea

Gilad's central argument is uncomfortable for most product teams: **most product work is based on opinion, not evidence**. Roadmaps are negotiated, not validated. Features ship because someone senior wanted them, not because they were tested against real user behavior.

The solution he proposes is the **GIST framework** — Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks — a four-layer hierarchy that forces you to separate what you're trying to achieve from how you plan to achieve it.

- **Goals** — measurable outcomes you want to reach (OKRs done right)
- **Ideas** — hypotheses about how to reach those goals
- **Steps** — minimum viable tests to validate each idea
- **Tasks** — actual execution work, only after an idea is validated

What makes this click is the order. Most teams jump straight from Goals to Tasks. GIST inserts Ideas and Steps as mandatory checkpoints. You can't ship something without having run at least one experiment on whether the idea is worth building.

## What I Actually Changed After Reading This

**I stopped treating roadmaps as commitments.** A roadmap is a hypothesis portfolio, not a delivery schedule. If the evidence changes, the roadmap changes. That shift in framing alone removed most of the tension I had with engineering and leadership around "missed commitments" on ideas that turned out to be wrong.

**I started using the Confidence Meter.** Every idea in the backlog now has an explicit confidence score — how confident are we that this will move the goal? Low confidence = cheap test first. High confidence = build. This made prioritization debates much more productive because we were arguing about evidence, not preference.

**I changed how I write opportunity statements.** Gilad insists on separating the problem from the solution at every layer. I rebuilt my discovery templates around this: we define what job the user is trying to do, measure the gap, then generate ideas. No solution-first thinking allowed until the problem is proven.

**I stopped confusing output with outcome.** Features shipped is an output. Retention improved is an outcome. The book hammered this distinction until it stuck permanently.

## What's Brilliant About It

The chapter on **ICE scoring** (Impact, Confidence, Ease) is worth the price of the book alone. It's a dead-simple prioritization framework that most teams can adopt in a single planning session. I've introduced it to three product teams and it immediately killed the "everything is P0" dysfunction.

The section on **step projects** is also underrated. Gilad proposes breaking big bets into a series of small validation steps — each one designed to either confirm the hypothesis or kill the idea cheaply. This is the operational version of the scientific method applied to product, and it maps beautifully to how I now structure discovery sprints.

## Where I'd Push Back

The framework assumes a reasonably mature data infrastructure. If you can't measure the metric you're trying to move, you can't run evidence-guided experiments. For early-stage products or companies without solid analytics, some of the advice is aspirational rather than immediately actionable.

There's also a slight tension with AI product development specifically. AI features often have emergent behavior that's hard to test in a clean, isolated step-project structure. The feedback loops are murkier, the metrics are noisier, and the right experiments require more setup. The book doesn't address AI development directly, so you'll need to adapt the framework to that context yourself.

## How Valuable Was It

This is a 5/5 for any product leader who has ever felt like their roadmap was more political document than product strategy. Gilad writes with the precision of someone who has watched the same dysfunction play out at hundreds of companies and finally decided to write down the fix.

It's not a book about innovation theatre or product intuition. It's a book about how to build a repeatable system for being right more often. In a world where most product decisions are still driven by the loudest voice in the room, that's a genuinely radical contribution.

**Read it if:** You lead product at a company where roadmaps feel like guesswork, discovery feels like theater, or you keep shipping features that don't move metrics.

**Skip it if:** You're pre-product-market-fit and still figuring out what problem you're solving — at that stage, speed of learning matters more than process.
