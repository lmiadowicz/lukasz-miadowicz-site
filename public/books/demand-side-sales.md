# Demand-Side Sales 101: Stop Selling and Help Your Customers Make Progress

## Why I Read It

I was running customer discovery interviews and kept getting answers that felt right but didn't help me make decisions. Customers would say "I'd love a feature that does X" and I'd build X and they wouldn't use it. Moesta's framing finally explained what I was doing wrong: I was capturing demand but not understanding it.

## The Core Idea

Moesta is one of the originators of the Jobs-to-be-Done framework alongside Clayton Christensen. This book applies that thinking specifically to the sales and buying process. The central idea: **people don't buy products, they hire products to help them make progress** in a specific struggling situation.

Every purchase follows a pattern:

**The struggling moment** - something happens in someone's life that creates tension between where they are and where they want to be. "My current process is taking too long and my boss is unhappy."

**The passive and active looking** - first they notice solutions passively (ads, word of mouth), then something tips them into actively searching.

**The decision** - they hire the new solution and fire the old one (the old solution could be a spreadsheet, a competitor, or doing nothing).

**Consumption** - they use the product in a context you probably didn't fully anticipate.

The **Four Forces** that drive every buying decision are: the push of the problem (why they're unhappy now), the pull of the new solution (what they imagine it will be like), the anxiety of the new (what could go wrong), and the attachment to the old (habit, sunk cost, familiarity). Sales - and product design - is really about managing these four forces.

## What I Actually Changed After Reading This

I rebuilt my customer interview framework. Instead of asking "what features do you want?", I now reconstruct the buying timeline: "Walk me through the moment you decided something needed to change. What was happening? What made that day different?" That backstory contains all the insight.

I started writing **job stories** instead of user stories for core flows. "When [struggling situation], I want to [progress I'm trying to make], so I can [outcome I'm hoping for]." This is strictly more useful than "As a [persona], I want [feature]" because it grounds the requirement in the actual demand context.

I changed how I think about **onboarding**. Most onboarding is designed around what the product does. Demand-side onboarding is designed around the struggling moment the user came from - it meets them where they are emotionally and helps them see the first sign that progress is happening.

## What's Brilliant About It

The chapter on **"Switch Interviews"** is the most practically useful part. Moesta's methodology for interviewing customers who recently switched from a competitor (or from doing nothing) is extremely detailed and immediately actionable. The questions are uncomfortable - you're asking people to narrate a bad experience - but the data is irreplaceable.

The distinction between **functional, emotional, and social jobs** is also well-handled. A PM tool doesn't just help you track tasks (functional) - it helps you feel in control (emotional) and look organised to your team (social). Neglecting the emotional and social layers is why so many technically sound products fail at adoption.

## Where I'd Push Back

The sales framing sometimes obscures the product insight. About half the book is aimed at sales practitioners, which is useful context but can feel like a detour if you're reading for product purposes.

The framework also works best for considered purchases - things where the buyer goes through a full decision process. For low-friction, impulse, or consumer products, the "struggling moment" narrative is hazier and the four forces are harder to map cleanly.

## How Valuable Was It

4/5. It's the clearest articulation of Jobs-to-be-Done I've read from an operational standpoint - not just the theory but the actual mechanics of how to do switch interviews and how to use the output. It directly improved my discovery process.

**Read it if:** You run customer research, write positioning, or want to understand why customers buy (or don't buy) your product in terms more useful than personas and demographics.

**Skip it if:** You're deep in execution mode and not doing active discovery. File it for when you're next in a research or positioning sprint.
